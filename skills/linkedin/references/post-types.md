# LinkedIn Post Types — Full Reference Library

All 12 post types with formula templates, 2-3 filled examples each, and common mistakes. Use the [bracketed] templates to create your own — then study the filled examples for tone and structure.

---

## 1. Personal Story Post

The most viral format on LinkedIn. People are wired for narrative. A good story creates emotional investment that carries the reader to your lesson.

### Formula

```
HOOK: [Dramatic contrast or vivid opening moment]

[2-3 lines of context — what was happening, set the scene]

[The struggle — specific, honest, vulnerable details]

[The turning point — what changed]

[What you learned — the universal insight]

[CTA: Question that invites their story]
```

### Template

```
[Time] ago I was [humble/difficult situation].

Today I [impressive outcome].

Here's what changed:

[Struggle detail 1]
[Struggle detail 2]
[Struggle detail 3]

Then [turning point happened].

[What you did differently]

[Result with specific metrics]

[Universal lesson — why this matters to anyone]

[Question: What's your version of this?]
```

### Example 1 — Career Pivot

```
3 years ago I got fired from my dream job.

My manager called me into a conference room at 4:47 PM on a Friday.

No warning. No PIP. Just: "We're going in a different direction."

I sat in my car for 40 minutes before I could drive home.

That night I made a list of everything I'd ignored:
→ The signs my work wasn't aligned with leadership's vision
→ The feedback I'd dismissed as "politics"
→ The relationships I hadn't invested in

I was so focused on being RIGHT that I forgot to be RELEVANT.

6 months later, I started my own consultancy.

Last year it did $840K in revenue.

Getting fired was the most expensive lesson I ever received.

And the most valuable.

What's your most painful career lesson?
```

### Example 2 — SaaS Founder

```
Last Tuesday, a customer called to cancel their $2,400/year subscription.

I asked one question: "What could we have done differently?"

She paused. Then said:

"Honestly? Nothing about your product. Your onboarding email felt like it was written by a robot. I never felt like a human cared if I succeeded."

That one sentence changed our entire approach.

We scrapped our automated sequence.
Replaced it with personal videos from our CS team.
30 seconds each. First name. Specific use case.

Results after 60 days:
→ Churn dropped 34%
→ NPS went from 31 to 67
→ Support tickets dropped 22%

The product didn't change. The feeling did.

People don't leave products. They leave experiences.

What's one "small" touch that made you loyal to a product?
```

### Common Mistakes
- Making the story about the outcome instead of the struggle (struggle is where the connection lives)
- Being vague — "things were tough" vs "I had $347 in my bank account"
- No universal lesson — the story needs to teach something anyone can apply
- Too long — keep it to one pivotal moment, not your entire life story

## 2. Product Launch Post

Announce a new product, feature, or service — but start with THEIR pain, not your product.

### Formula

```
HOOK: [Describe the pain point your audience knows too well]

[Validate the pain — show you understand it deeply]

[Reveal what you built / launched]

[How it solves the specific pain — be concrete]

[Early results or social proof if available]

[CTA: Who else has this problem? / Link in comments]
```

### Template

```
[Common frustrating experience your audience has].

I've [experienced/seen/heard] this [number] times.

Every time, the same thought: "There has to be a better way."

So we built one.

[Product/feature name] does [specific thing] in [specific way].

[Early result: metric or user quote]

[What makes it different from existing solutions]

If you've ever [pain point], this is for you.

Link in comments 👇
```

### Example 1 — Tool Launch

```
I booked a demo call with a tool rated 4.8/5 on G2.

The "demo" was a 45-minute slideshow.

No product. No live walkthrough. No answers to my specific questions.

I've sat through 200+ vendor demos in the last 3 years.

80% of them follow the same playbook: slides → features nobody asked about → "any questions?" at minute 44.

We built [your product] differently.

Every demo is live. In your actual environment. With your real data.

No slides. No scripts. Just your use case, solved in 25 minutes.

Our close rate went from 12% to 34% in 90 days.

The best demo isn't the one that shows the most features.

It's the one that solves one problem completely.

If you're tired of slide-deck demos, link in comments 👇
```

### Example 2 — Feature Launch

```
Every founder I talk to has the same complaint:

"I have 4 analytics dashboards open and I STILL don't know what's working."

We felt the same pain.

So we built [feature name] — one screen that shows:
→ Which channel brought your last 10 customers
→ Your real CAC (not the spreadsheet guess)
→ Revenue by cohort, updated live

No imports. No setup. Connects in 90 seconds.

Early users are saving 3+ hours per week on reporting.

One founder told us: "This is the first dashboard I actually check every morning."

If you're drowning in dashboards, link in comments 👇
```

### Common Mistakes
- Leading with your product name (nobody cares about your product — they care about their pain)
- Feature-dumping instead of showing one clear transformation
- Sounding like a press release ("We're thrilled to announce...")
- Putting the link in the post body (always use first comment)

## 3. Hiring Post

You need a team member. "I don't have [crucial role]" outperforms "We're hiring [role]" dramatically.

### Formula

```
HOOK: "I don't have [crucial missing capability]."

[Why this matters — the cost of not having this person]

[What the role actually looks like — be honest and specific]

[What you offer — beyond compensation]

[What kind of person thrives here]

[CTA: Know someone? Tag them / DM me / Link in comments]
```

### Template

```
I don't have [crucial missing role/capability].

And it's costing us [specific consequence].

Here's what I need:

Someone who [key responsibility 1].
Someone who [key responsibility 2].
Someone who [key responsibility 3].

What I can offer:
→ [Unique benefit 1]
→ [Unique benefit 2]
→ [Honest reality about the role]

You don't need [traditional requirement].
You need [what actually matters].

Know someone? Tag them below.
Or DM me. Seriously.
```

### Example 1

```
I don't have a right hand.

I'm a solo founder doing sales, product, and customer support. Simultaneously.

It's not sustainable. Something is always on fire.

I need a co-pilot who can:
→ Talk to customers without a script
→ Make decisions without asking me
→ Build process from zero (we don't have SOPs yet)

What I offer:
→ Equity. Real equity. Not 0.1%
→ Direct impact — you'll shape everything
→ Brutal honesty about where we are (early, messy, exciting)

You don't need an MBA.
You need the ability to figure things out with duct tape and determination.

If this sounds like you (or someone you know), DM me.
```

### Common Mistakes
- Writing a job description instead of a story
- "We're looking for a rockstar" — meaningless
- Not being honest about the hard parts (people respect transparency)
- Too formal — hiring posts that sound human dramatically outperform corporate job listings

## 4. Thought Leadership / Hot Take

Share a bold opinion that's controversial but defensible. Your filter on the world.

### Formula

```
HOOK: "[Controversial statement that challenges common wisdom]"

[What everyone else believes]

[Why you disagree — with evidence]

[Your alternative perspective — with proof]

[The nuance — you're not dismissing everything]

[CTA: Agree or disagree?]
```

### Template

```
"[Common advice/belief]" — worst advice I've ever heard.

Here's why:

Everyone says [popular thing].

But in [your experience/data]:
→ [Counter-evidence 1]
→ [Counter-evidence 2]
→ [Counter-evidence 3]

What actually works: [your alternative]

I'm not saying [common thing] is always wrong.

I'm saying blindly following it is dangerous.

[Question: What's the worst advice you've received in your field?]
```

### Example 1 — Startup Advice

```
"Raise money as fast as possible" — worst advice I've ever heard.

Everyone celebrates the raise. Nobody talks about:

→ The 67% of VC-backed startups that never return investor capital
→ The founders who lost control of their own company by Series B
→ The pressure to grow at all costs, even when the market isn't ready

I bootstrapped to $2M ARR. Took 3 years. Was painful.

But I own 100% of my company.
I have zero board meetings.
I make decisions in minutes, not months.

VC is jet fuel. It's incredible if you're already on the runway.

But most founders pour jet fuel on a skateboard and wonder why it explodes.

Build the plane first. Then decide if you need the fuel.

Agree or disagree? I'd genuinely love to hear the other side.
```

### Example 2 — Productivity

```
Stop waking up at 5 AM.

Every LinkedIn guru says early mornings = success.

Here's what they don't tell you:

I forced myself to wake up at 5 AM for 6 months.

Result:
→ I was exhausted by 2 PM
→ My best creative work happens at 10 PM (always has)
→ I was optimizing for discipline theater, not actual output

My output increased 40% when I stopped fighting my chronotype.

Only ~25% of people are natural early risers.

The question isn't WHEN you work. It's whether you're doing deep work during YOUR peak hours.

Stop copying someone else's schedule. Design your own.

Are you a morning person or a night owl? No judgment.
```

### Common Mistakes
- Being contrarian without evidence (hot take without substance = cheap engagement)
- Attacking people instead of ideas
- Not acknowledging the other side (best contrarian posts show nuance)
- Taking a "controversial" position that everyone actually agrees with (fake contrarian)

## 5. Tech/Industry News Commentary

React to industry news with YOUR unique filter. Don't just restate the news — add perspective.

### Formula

```
HOOK: [The news headline, reframed through your lens]

[What happened — brief context]

[What everyone is saying about it]

[What most people are MISSING]

[Your take — what this actually means]

[CTA: What's your read on this?]
```

### Template

```
[Company/Product] just [did something].

Everyone is talking about [the obvious angle].

But here's what nobody is saying:

[Your unique insight 1]
[Your unique insight 2]

This isn't about [surface topic].

This is about [deeper implication].

If you're in [industry/role], here's what this means for you:
→ [Practical implication 1]
→ [Practical implication 2]

[Question about the news]
```

### Example 1

```
OpenAI just dropped their latest model.

Everyone is talking about the benchmarks.

Here's what nobody is saying:

The benchmarks don't matter for 90% of businesses.

What matters is:
→ The API cost dropped 40% (your margins just improved)
→ The context window doubled (you can now process entire documents)
→ Fine-tuning is 3x faster (custom models in hours, not days)

If you're building with AI, ignore the Twitter hype.

Open your cost spreadsheet instead.

The real AI revolution isn't smarter models.

It's cheaper ones.

What's your team using AI for right now? I'm collecting use cases.
```

### Common Mistakes
- Just restating the news (add YOUR perspective or don't post)
- Being late (if the news is 3+ days old, you've missed the window)
- Hot-taking on topics outside your expertise
- Not connecting the news to your audience's daily reality

## 6. Milestone Post

Share wins authentically. The secret: the journey is more interesting than the destination.

### Formula

```
HOOK: [The milestone — state it clearly]

[But here's what that number doesn't show:]

[The journey — specific timeline with honest details]

[What nearly killed you along the way]

[What actually made the difference]

[Universal lesson]

[CTA: Encouragement + question]
```

### Template

```
[Big milestone achieved].

But here's what that number doesn't show:

→ [Month/Phase 1]: [Humble beginning detail]
→ [Month/Phase 2]: [Struggle detail]
→ [Month/Phase 3]: [Turning point detail]
→ [Current]: [Where you are now]

What nearly killed us:
- [Specific near-death experience 1]
- [Specific near-death experience 2]

[Milestone] sounds like a destination.

It's actually just proof that you survived long enough.

[Encouragement for people earlier in the journey]

[Question about their journey]
```

### Example 1

```
We just hit $1M ARR.

But here's what that number doesn't show:

→ Month 1-6: $0 in revenue. Just building.
→ Month 7: First customer. $49/month. I literally screamed.
→ Month 12: $4K MRR. Barely covering server costs.
→ Month 18: $22K MRR. First full-time hire.
→ Month 24: $83K MRR. Today.

What nearly killed us:
- Running out of money in month 9 (took a freelance gig to fund the company)
- Losing our biggest customer in month 14 (20% of revenue, gone overnight)
- A 3-week outage in month 16 that almost destroyed our reputation

$1M ARR sounds like a destination.

It's actually just proof that you survived long enough.

To everyone in months 1-6 with $0 revenue: keep going.

The hardest part isn't building the product. It's believing in it when nobody else does yet.
```

### Common Mistakes
- Humble-bragging ("I can't believe it!", "I'm so humbled!")
- Just announcing the number without the story behind it
- Not sharing the hard parts (the struggle is what makes the milestone meaningful)
- Making it about you instead of making it inspirational for others

## 7. Educational / How-To Post

Step-by-step content that delivers immediate, actionable value. Positions you as an expert.

### Formula

```
HOOK: How to [achieve desirable outcome] (step by step):

Step 1: [Action]
→ [Why and how — 1-2 lines]

Step 2: [Action]
→ [Why and how — 1-2 lines]

...

[Bonus tip or common mistake to avoid]

[CTA: Save this for later / Try this and tell me what happens]
```

### Example 1

```
How to write a LinkedIn hook that stops the scroll (step by step):

Step 1: Start with the end
→ What's the ONE takeaway? Write that first. Now work backwards.

Step 2: Find the tension
→ What's surprising, counterintuitive, or emotionally charged about your point?

Step 3: Use a proven structure
→ "[Specific number] + [unexpected insight]" always works.
→ Example: "I spent $47K on courses. Only one was worth it."

Step 4: Cut every unnecessary word
→ Your hook should be under 20 words. Ideally under 12.

Step 5: Read it as a stranger
→ If it doesn't make YOU want to click "see more" — rewrite it.

The difference between 500 impressions and 50,000?

Usually just the first 2 lines.

Try this framework on your next post and tell me what happens.
```

### Example 2

```
How to cold email an investor and actually get a response:

Step 1: Research the investor for 10 minutes
→ Find a portfolio company similar to yours. Reference it specifically.

Step 2: Subject line = 3-5 words
→ "[Mutual connection] intro" or "[Company name] — [one metric]"

Step 3: First sentence = why THEM
→ "I saw you led [Company X]'s seed round. We're solving a similar problem in [adjacent space]."

Step 4: One paragraph = what you do + traction
→ "[Product] helps [customer] do [outcome]. We're at [$X MRR] growing [Y]% MoM."

Step 5: The ask = specific and small
→ "Would you have 15 minutes this week?"

Total length: under 100 words.

I've sent ~300 of these. Response rate: 31%.

The secret isn't the template. It's the research in Step 1.

Would a follow-up post on what to include in the one-pager be helpful?
```

### Common Mistakes
- Too many steps (5-7 is the sweet spot, more than 10 loses attention)
- Vague steps ("Build a strategy" — how? Be specific)
- No proof that this approach actually works
- All theory, no practical example

## 8. Case Study Post

Real results with specific details that prove expertise. Before/after structure.

### Formula

```
HOOK: [Client/project] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe].

Here's exactly what we did:

THE PROBLEM:
[Specific situation — make it relatable]

THE APPROACH:
1. [What you did — be tactical]
2. [What you did]
3. [What you did]

THE RESULTS:
→ [Specific metric 1]
→ [Specific metric 2]
→ [Specific metric 3]

THE LESSON:
[What this teaches about the broader topic]

[CTA: What's your experience with this?]
```

### Example 1

```
A B2B SaaS company came to us with 2% trial-to-paid conversion.

Industry average: 15-25%.

Here's exactly what we changed:

THE PROBLEM:
Their 14-day trial had zero guidance. Users signed up, saw a dashboard, and left.

THE APPROACH:
1. Added a 3-step onboarding checklist (complete in <5 min)
2. Triggered a personal email from the CEO at hour 24
3. Showed "time saved" metrics inside the product after first use
4. Reduced trial from 14 days to 7 (created urgency)

THE RESULTS (after 90 days):
→ Trial-to-paid: 2% → 18%
→ Time to first value: 3 days → 47 minutes
→ Monthly revenue: +$34K

The lesson:

A trial isn't a "free sample." It's a guided experience.

If users don't reach the "aha moment" in the first session, they never will.

What's your trial conversion rate? I'll share what I'd test.
```

### Example 2

```
A client came to us with $50K/month in ad spend and a 0.8x ROAS.

They were burning $50K/month to make $40K.

Their previous agency's solution? "We need to test more creatives."

We looked at the data. The creatives were fine.

The problem? They were sending traffic to a homepage with 11 CTAs, 3 pop-ups, and a loading time of 7.2 seconds.

We didn't touch the ads. We rebuilt the landing page.

One CTA. Zero pop-ups. 1.4 second load time.

ROAS went from 0.8x to 3.2x in 3 weeks.

Your ads aren't broken. Your landing page is.

Stop optimizing the top of the funnel when the bottom has a hole in it.

Anyone else seen this pattern?
```

### Common Mistakes
- No specific numbers (vague case studies convince nobody)
- Sounding like a sales pitch instead of a teaching moment
- Not getting permission to share the results
- Skipping the "lesson" — the case study should teach, not just flex

## 9. Behind the Scenes Post

Pull back the curtain on real work. Authenticity > polish.

### Formula

```
HOOK: Here's what [process/project] actually looks like:

[Day-by-day or step-by-step honest breakdown]

[What went wrong / what was harder than expected]

[What you'd do differently]

[The real lesson most people won't share]

[CTA: Any others want to share their real version?]
```

### Example 1

```
Here's what building a startup actually looks like:

Monday: Closed our biggest deal ever ($48K ACV). Team celebrated.

Tuesday: Our main API provider changed their pricing. Margins went from 62% to 31% overnight.

Wednesday: Emergency meeting. Rewrote our entire cost model.

Thursday: Lost one of 3 customers we'd emailed about price changes. The $48K deal? They want a 30% discount now.

Friday: Fixed the API cost issue with an open-source alternative. Took our lead engineer 14 hours straight.

This was last week. Every week looks like this.

The LinkedIn highlight reel shows the wins. The reality is wins surrounded by fires.

If you're building something and it feels chaotic — that's not failure.

That's what building looks like.

Any other founders want to share their "real week"?
```

### Example 2

```
I posted on LinkedIn every weekday for 90 days.

Here's the honest breakdown:

Posts written: 90
Posts I was proud of: ~12
Posts that flopped: ~40
Posts that went viral (10K+ impressions): 7

My most-planned, most-researched post? 847 impressions.
My "I wrote this in 8 minutes at midnight" post? 67K impressions.

What I actually learned:
→ Consistency > quality (you can't predict what hits)
→ Personal stories outperform "value" posts 3:1
→ Commenting on others' posts drove more followers than my own posts
→ The best ideas come from conversations, not brainstorming

Would I do it again? Absolutely.

But I'd stress less about each individual post.

What's your experience with consistent posting?
```

### Common Mistakes
- Manufacturing "mess" (audiences detect fake vulnerability instantly)
- Being so raw it's uncomfortable (vulnerable, not therapy)
- No lesson at the end (behind-the-scenes without insight is just a diary)

## 10. Question/Poll Post

Spark discussion by asking something people have strong opinions about. The question must be genuinely debatable.

### Formula

```
HOOK: [Provocative or thought-provoking question]

[Your take — take a SIDE, don't fence-sit]

[Why you believe this]

[Acknowledge the other perspective]

[CTA: But I might be wrong. What's your experience?]
```

### Template

```
[Specific debatable question]?

I've been thinking about this a lot.

My take: [clear position]

Here's why:
→ [Reason 1]
→ [Reason 2]
→ [Reason 3]

But I know smart people who disagree.

What's YOUR experience? I genuinely want to hear both sides.
```

### Example 1

```
Would you take a 40% pay cut to work on something you love?

I did. Twice.

First time: Left a $180K corporate job to join a 4-person startup at $108K.
Result: The startup failed in 11 months.

Second time: Left a $160K role to start my own company at $0.
Result: Took 14 months to match my old salary. Now earning 3x.

Both bets were worth it.

The corporate job was slowly making me someone I didn't recognize.

Money is replaceable. Time and identity are not.

But I know this take isn't universal. What would you do?
```

### Common Mistakes
- Asking vague questions ("Thoughts?")
- Questions with obvious answers (no debate = no comments)
- Not sharing your own position first (fence-sitting kills engagement)
- "Like if A, comment if B" (engagement bait, algorithm penalizes)

## 11. Listicle Post

Numbered insights that are easy to consume and save. The numbered format creates a commitment loop.

### Formula

```
HOOK: [X] things I learned about [topic] after [credibility marker]:

1. [Insight] — [1-line explanation]

2. [Insight] — [1-line explanation]

...

[CTA: Which one resonates most? / Save this]
```

### Example 1

```
9 things I learned after 1,200+ sales calls:

1. The best salespeople listen 70% of the time. Average ones talk 70%.

2. "What's your budget?" is a terrible question. Ask "What would it cost you to NOT solve this?"

3. Follow up 5 times minimum. 80% of deals close after the 5th touch.

4. Never send a proposal without a verbal yes first.

5. The decision-maker is rarely the person in the first meeting.

6. Objections are buying signals. Silence is the real rejection.

7. Your competitor isn't another vendor — it's "do nothing."

8. Case studies close more deals than features ever will.

9. The deal is won or lost in the discovery call. Everything after is confirmation.

Which one surprised you?

I'd add #10 but I'll save it for the comments.
```

### Example 2

```
7 UX lessons that took me 10 years to learn:

1. Users don't read. They scan. Design for F-pattern, not novels.

2. The best feature is the one you remove.

3. "Intuitive" means "works like something I've used before."

4. Loading states aren't optional. Uncertainty causes abandonment.

5. Your power users are 5% of your base. Stop designing for them first.

6. Every extra form field costs you 7-10% of conversions.

7. Accessibility isn't a nice-to-have. 15% of the world has a disability.

Bonus: If you have to explain how it works, it doesn't work.

Save this. You'll come back to it.
```

### Common Mistakes
- Too many items (5-10 is the sweet spot, 15+ loses attention)
- Generic insights anyone could write (your unique experience is the value)
- No personal commentary (just a list without YOUR take is ChatGPT-level)
- Items are all the same depth (vary: some short, some with a mini-story)

## 12. Contrarian / Myth-Busting Post

Challenge a widespread belief with evidence. These generate strong reactions from both sides.

### Formula

```
HOOK: "[Common belief]" — or "Most people think [X]. They're wrong."

[What everyone believes and why]

[Your counter-evidence — specific, not theoretical]

[What actually works instead]

[Why the myth persists]

[CTA: Agree or disagree?]
```

### Example 1

```
"You need to post every day on LinkedIn to grow."

No. You don't.

I grew from 2K to 47K followers posting 3x per week.

Here's what actually matters more than frequency:

1. Consistency > volume (3x/week for 6 months beats daily for 6 weeks)
2. Quality of engagement (commenting on others' posts for 20 min/day)
3. Hook quality (one great hook > five mediocre posts)
4. Niche focus (being known for ONE thing compounds faster)

The "post daily" advice benefits LinkedIn, not you.

The algorithm rewards relevance, not frequency.

I'd rather write 3 posts that make people think than 7 posts that fill space.

What's your posting frequency? And has more/less worked for you?
```

### Example 2

```
"Culture fit" is the most dangerous phrase in hiring.

Here's what it actually means 90% of the time:

"This person reminds me of myself."

Same background. Same communication style. Same references.

That's not culture fit. That's confirmation bias wearing a nice shirt.

The best teams I've built had:
→ An introvert who caught every detail others missed
→ A "disagreeable" engineer who prevented 3 catastrophic launches
→ A career-changer who brought frameworks from a completely different industry

They didn't "fit" the culture. They made it better.

Hire for values alignment, not vibes.

What's the worst "culture fit" decision you've seen?
```

### Common Mistakes
- Contrarian without evidence (just being edgy, not insightful)
- Not acknowledging when the common belief IS sometimes right (nuance matters)
- Attacking people who hold the belief (attack the idea, not the person)
- "Controversial" take that everyone actually agrees with

## Post Type Selection Guide

Use this to quickly pick the right post type:

| If you have... | Use... |
|---------------|--------|
| A personal experience with a lesson | Story Post (#1) |
| A new product/feature to announce | Product Launch (#2) |
| An open role to fill | Hiring Post (#3) |
| A strong opinion backed by evidence | Thought Leadership (#4) |
| A reaction to industry news | Tech/News Commentary (#5) |
| A win or achievement to share | Milestone (#6) |
| A process to teach step by step | Educational/How-To (#7) |
| Client results with metrics | Case Study (#8) |
| The messy reality of your work | Behind the Scenes (#9) |
| A question you want debated | Question/Poll (#10) |
| Multiple related insights | Listicle (#11) |
| A popular belief you disagree with | Contrarian/Myth-Busting (#12) |
