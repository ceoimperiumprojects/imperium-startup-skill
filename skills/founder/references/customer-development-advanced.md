# Customer Development — Advanced

Steve Blank's 4-step model, getting out of the building, market types, and systematic customer learning. Distilled from "The Startup Owner's Manual" and Lean Startup methodology.

## The Four Steps to the Epiphany

Steve Blank's core insight: startups are NOT small versions of big companies. They are temporary organizations searching for a repeatable, scalable business model.

```
┌─────────────┐   ┌─────────────┐   ┌─────────────┐   ┌─────────────┐
│  Customer    │──▶│  Customer    │──▶│  Customer    │──▶│  Company     │
│  Discovery   │   │  Validation  │   │  Creation    │   │  Building    │
└─────────────┘   └─────────────┘   └─────────────┘   └─────────────┘
     SEARCH              SEARCH          EXECUTION         EXECUTION
```

### Step 1: Customer Discovery

**Goal:** Do people have this problem? Is it painful enough to solve?

**Activities:**
- State hypotheses about business model (who, what, why, how)
- Test problem hypotheses (interviews, observation)
- Test solution hypotheses (demos, MVPs)
- Pivot or proceed

**Key question:** "Is there a problem worth solving?"

**Output:** Problem-solution fit documented with evidence

### Step 2: Customer Validation

**Goal:** Can we sell this? Is the business model repeatable?

**Activities:**
- Sell to "earlyvangelists" (early adopters who actively seek solutions)
- Validate sales roadmap and process
- Validate channel strategy
- Pivot or proceed

**Key question:** "Will people pay for the solution?"

**Output:** Proven sales process, repeatable revenue

### Step 3: Customer Creation

**Goal:** Create end-user demand and drive it into the sales channel.

**Activities depend on market type (see below)**

### Step 4: Company Building

**Goal:** Transition from learning organization to execution organization.

**Activities:**
- Build departments, management layers
- Scale what works from validation phase
- DO NOT skip steps 1-2

## Market Types (Critical Strategic Choice)

Market type determines EVERYTHING about go-to-market strategy:

| Factor | Existing Market | Re-segmented | New Market |
|--------|----------------|--------------|------------|
| **Customers** | Known | Known, different need | Unknown |
| **Competition** | Many, known | Known, different angle | None (yet) |
| **Risk** | Execution | Positioning | Adoption |
| **Time to revenue** | Months | Months-Year | Years |
| **Sales strategy** | Features, performance | Niche + value | Education, evangelism |
| **Marketing spend** | High (compete) | Moderate (niche) | Low early, high later |
| **Example** | Better CRM | CRM for dentists | First smartphone |

**How to identify your market type:**
- Are users already spending money on this problem? → Existing
- Are you targeting an underserved niche of an existing market? → Re-segmented
- Does no one know they need this yet? → New market

## The Earlyvangelists

The most important customer segment. Characteristics:
1. **Has the problem** (and knows it)
2. **Has been actively seeking a solution** (not waiting)
3. **Has cobbled together a partial solution** (spreadsheets, manual processes)
4. **Has budget** (or can get it)
5. **Can serve as a reference** (willing to be public)

**Why they matter:** Earlyvangelists buy unfinished products, forgive bugs, and give brutally honest feedback. They are your first revenue AND your product development team.

**How to find them:**
- Who is Googling for solutions right now?
- Who is in forums/communities complaining about this problem?
- Who has built internal tools to solve it?
- Who is your competitor's most frustrated customer?

## Getting Out of the Building (GOOB)

Blank's most famous principle. No business plan survives first contact with customers.

**GOOB rules:**
1. Founders do the interviews, not delegates
2. Minimum 10-15 interviews per customer segment
3. In-person > video > phone > survey (always)
4. Ask about behavior, not opinions
5. Document within 24 hours

**Interview framework (Problem Interview):**
```
1. Welcome (2 min)     — Set context, get permission to take notes
2. Demographics (3 min) — Validate they match your target segment
3. Tell me a story (10 min) — "Walk me through the last time you..."
4. Problem ranking (5 min) — "Of these problems, which is most painful?"
5. Explore (5 min)     — Dig into the top problem
6. Wrap up (2 min)     — Ask for referrals
```

**What to listen for (validation signals):**
- They describe the problem with emotion
- They've tried to solve it (spent time or money)
- They ask when your solution will be ready
- They offer to pay / commit time for pilot
- They introduce you to others with the same problem

## Pivot Decision Framework

After each batch of 10-15 interviews:

| Signal | Action |
|--------|--------|
| 8+ confirm the problem is severe | Proceed to solution testing |
| 5-7 confirm, mixed severity | Narrow the segment, re-test |
| <5 confirm | Pivot: different problem or different customer |
| Problem confirmed but won't pay | Pivot: different business model or segment |
| Everyone says "nice to have" | Kill it — "nice to have" = won't pay |

## Common Customer Development Mistakes

1. **Confirmation bias** — Only hearing what supports your idea
2. **Leading questions** — "Don't you think X would be great?" (always gets yes)
3. **Too many features shown** — Show the minimum, observe the reaction
4. **Interviewing friends** — They'll be nice, not honest
5. **No documentation** — If you didn't write it down, it didn't happen
6. **Skipping to Customer Creation** — The #1 startup killer is premature scaling
