# Negotiation Tactics

FBI-level negotiation techniques for sales, partnerships, and fundraising. Distilled from Chris Voss' "Never Split the Difference" (227 chunks in knowledge base).

## Core Philosophy

Negotiation is not about logic — it's about emotions. The person who best understands and influences the emotional landscape wins. "He who has learned to disagree without being disagreeable has discovered the most valuable secret of negotiation."

## Tactical Empathy

**Definition:** Understanding the other side's feelings and mindset, then demonstrating that understanding.

**Not sympathy** (feeling sorry for them). Not agreement. Just understanding.

**How to use:**
- Listen with intent to understand, not to respond
- Identify what they're afraid of losing
- Verbalize their perspective before stating yours
- "It seems like this is really important to you because..."

**Why it works:** When people feel understood, their guard drops. They become collaborative instead of combative.

## The 7 Core Techniques

### 1. Mirroring

Repeat the last 1-3 critical words they just said. Then go silent.

**Example:**
- Them: "We just can't go above $50K for this."
- You: "Can't go above $50K?"
- Them: *elaborates, often revealing the real constraint*

**Why it works:** Creates rapport. Triggers elaboration. Costs you nothing. Use 4-6 times per conversation.

### 2. Labeling

Name the emotion you observe: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It looks like..."

**Examples:**
- "It seems like you're concerned about implementation timeline."
- "It sounds like you've been burned by vendors before."
- "It looks like budget approval is the real blocker here."

**Rules:**
- Never say "I" — use "It seems/sounds/looks"
- Pause after labeling. Let them confirm or correct.
- Label negatives to diffuse them, positives to reinforce them.

### 3. Calibrated Questions

Open-ended "How" and "What" questions that give the other side the illusion of control:

**Power questions:**
- "How am I supposed to do that?" (soft no without saying no)
- "What about this is important to you?"
- "How would you like me to proceed?"
- "What happens if we don't reach an agreement?"
- "How does this affect the rest of your team?"
- "What's the biggest challenge you face with this?"

**Never ask "Why"** — it sounds accusatory. Replace with "What caused..." or "What led to..."

### 4. Accusation Audit

Proactively list every negative thing they might think about you BEFORE they say it:

**Example (sales pitch opening):**
"You probably think I'm just another vendor trying to sell you something you don't need. You might be worried about implementation disruption. You might think the ROI numbers I show are inflated..."

**Why it works:** Takes the sting out of their objections before they even raise them. They often respond with "No, it's not that bad..."

### 5. The Late-Night FM DJ Voice

Slow down. Lower your tone. Speak with downward inflection (declarative, not questioning).

**When to use:** Delivering price, making a point, defusing tension.
**When NOT to use:** Building rapport (use upbeat, positive tone instead).

### 6. "No"-Oriented Questions

People feel safe saying "no" and threatened by "yes." Flip your questions:

| Instead of (Yes-oriented) | Ask (No-oriented) |
|---------------------------|-------------------|
| "Do you agree this is a good deal?" | "Is it a ridiculous idea to..." |
| "Can we move forward?" | "Have you given up on this project?" |
| "Is this a good time?" | "Is now a bad time to talk?" |
| "Do you want to save money?" | "Would it be terrible if you saved $50K?" |

### 7. "That's Right" (The Breakthrough Moment)

Your goal is to get them to say **"That's right"** — not "You're right" (which means "shut up").

**How to trigger "That's right":**
1. Listen carefully to their position
2. Summarize their perspective back to them (paraphrase + label)
3. They say "That's right" = they feel fully understood
4. NOW you can make your proposal

## The Ackerman Bargaining Model

Systematic approach to price/salary negotiation:

```
1. Set your target price
2. First offer: 65% of target
3. Calculate three raises: 85%, 95%, 100%
4. Use empathy + calibrated questions between each raise
5. Final number: precise, non-round (e.g., $47,850 not $48,000)
6. On final offer, throw in a non-monetary item they don't want
```

**Why precise numbers:** $47,850 sounds calculated and thoughtful. $48,000 sounds like you picked a round number. Precise = credible.

## Black Swans

Information the other side has that would change everything if you knew it. Every negotiation has 3-5.

**How to find them:**
- Ask open-ended questions and actually listen
- Pay attention to what they DON'T say
- Watch for off-hand comments and emotional reactions
- Meet in person (body language reveals black swans)

**Example black swans:**
- They have a deadline you don't know about
- They've already been burned by your competitor
- Their budget actually refreshes next month
- The decision maker changed last week

## Sales-Specific Application

### Discovery Call
1. Open with accusation audit: "You probably get 50 cold calls a week..."
2. Mirror their problem statements
3. Label their emotions about the problem
4. Use calibrated questions to deepen understanding
5. Get "that's right" on their situation summary

### Price Negotiation
1. Anchor with Ackerman model
2. When they push back: "How am I supposed to do that?" (calibrated question)
3. Label their budget concerns
4. Ask "What would you need to see to justify this investment?"
5. Never split the difference — find creative value adds instead

### Closing
- "It seems like we've covered everything important to you. Is there anything I've missed?"
- "What happens if you don't solve this problem in the next quarter?"
- "Would it be a terrible idea to start with a pilot next month?"

## Anti-Patterns

- **Splitting the difference** — Never compromise. Find creative solutions that address both sides' needs.
- **"Yes" collecting** — Getting people to say yes to small things doesn't make them say yes to big things. It makes them feel manipulated.
- **Talking too much** — Aim for 30/70 split. You talk 30%, they talk 70%.
- **Revealing your deadline** — Time pressure is leverage. Never give yours away.
