# Strategic Analysis Frameworks

## SWOT Analysis

| | Helpful | Harmful |
|---|---------|---------|
| **Internal** | Strengths | Weaknesses |
| **External** | Opportunities | Threats |

**How to use:**
- Be specific — "strong team" is useless, "3 engineers with ML experience" is useful
- Weight each item by impact (High/Medium/Low)
- Create action items: Leverage strengths, address weaknesses, seize opportunities, mitigate threats
- Update quarterly — the landscape changes

## Porter's Five Forces

1. **Competitive Rivalry:** How intense is competition? (Number of competitors, differentiation, exit barriers)
2. **Threat of New Entrants:** How easy to enter? (Capital requirements, regulation, brand loyalty, economies of scale)
3. **Threat of Substitutes:** Are there alternatives? (Switching costs, relative price-performance, buyer propensity)
4. **Bargaining Power of Buyers:** How much leverage do customers have? (Concentration, switching costs, price sensitivity)
5. **Bargaining Power of Suppliers:** How much leverage do suppliers have? (Number of suppliers, uniqueness, switching costs)

## First Principles Thinking

1. Identify the problem clearly
2. Break it down to fundamental truths (what do we KNOW, not assume?)
3. Separate facts from assumptions
4. Build up a solution from the fundamentals
5. Test against conventional wisdom — where do they diverge?

## Scenario Planning

### Three-scenario model:

**Bear Case:** What if things go wrong?
- Key assumptions that could fail
- Impact on revenue, runway, team
- Mitigation plans and decision triggers

**Base Case:** Most likely outcome
- Conservative but realistic assumptions
- Budget and plan based on this
- Monthly tracking against this case

**Bull Case:** What if things go right?
- What would accelerate growth?
- What resources would we need?
- What decisions should we make now to enable this?

### Decision triggers:
At each monthly review, check:
- Are we tracking to Bear, Base, or Bull?
- Have any assumptions changed?
- Do we need to adjust the plan?

## Strategic Planning: OKR Framework

**Objectives:** What we want to achieve (qualitative, inspiring)
**Key Results:** How we measure progress (quantitative, measurable)

**Rules:**
- 3-5 objectives per quarter (focus!)
- 2-4 key results per objective
- Key results must be measurable (number, %, milestone)
- 70% achievement = good (if you hit 100%, goals were too easy)
- Review weekly, grade quarterly

**Example:**
```
Objective: Achieve product-market fit for Tunia
  KR1: Reach 500 weekly active users (from 50)
  KR2: Day 7 retention > 40% (from 15%)
  KR3: NPS score > 40 (from unmeasured)
  KR4: 10 paid subscribers (from 0)
```

## Capital Allocation Framework

### Priority Levels

1. **Essential (40-50%):** Core operations, compliance, security — without this, the company dies
2. **Strategic (30-40%):** Growth initiatives — the bets that grow the business
3. **Efficiency (10-15%):** Cost reduction, automation — doing more with less
4. **Experimental (5-10%):** Innovation, R&D — new ideas with uncertain outcomes

### Budget Decision Tree

```
Is it required for operations?
├─ Yes → Essential (auto-approve if <$X)
└─ No → Does it drive revenue growth?
    ├─ Yes → Expected ROI?
    │   ├─ >30% → Strategic (approve with tracking)
    │   └─ <30% → Defer or negotiate
    └─ No → Does it reduce costs?
        ├─ Yes → Payback period?
        │   ├─ <12 months → Efficiency (approve)
        │   └─ >12 months → Defer
        └─ No → Experimental (limited budget, requires hypothesis)
```

## Stakeholder Mapping

| Quadrant | Interest Low | Interest High |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Power High** | Keep satisfied | Manage closely |
| **Power Low** | Monitor | Keep informed |

For each key stakeholder:
- What do they care about?
- What do they need from you?
- How often should you communicate?
- What's the best channel?
