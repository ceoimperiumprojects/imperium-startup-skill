# Product Positioning — Advanced

April Dunford's 5-step positioning process, competitive alternatives, and best-fit customer strategy. Distilled from "Obviously Awesome" (77 chunks in knowledge base).

## Why Positioning Matters

Positioning is the context that makes your product make sense. Without it, customers can't figure out what you are, who it's for, or why they should care.

**"Positioning is not about what you say about your product. It's about how you frame it so customers immediately understand its value."**

**Bad positioning symptoms:**
- "What exactly do you do?" from prospects
- Being compared to the wrong competitors
- Long, confusing sales cycles
- Customers using your product for something you didn't intend

## The 5-Step Positioning Process

### Step 1: Identify Your Competitive Alternatives

**Question:** "What would customers do if your product didn't exist?"

This is NOT "who are your competitors." It's broader:
- Existing software solutions
- Manual processes (spreadsheets, pen and paper)
- Hiring someone to do it
- **Doing nothing** (often the biggest competitor)

**Example:**
- You think your competitor is Salesforce
- But customers would actually just use spreadsheets
- Your positioning should be vs spreadsheets, not vs Salesforce

**How to find competitive alternatives:**
- Ask recent customers: "What were you doing before?"
- Ask churned customers: "What did you switch to?"
- Look at how prospects describe their current solution

### Step 2: Identify Your Unique Attributes

**Question:** "What do you have that alternatives don't?"

List EVERYTHING that's different (not better — different):
- Features or capabilities
- Technology approach
- Business model
- Delivery method
- Expertise or focus area
- Integrations

**Rules:**
- Only list things that are OBJECTIVELY different
- Not "better UX" (subjective) but "drag-and-drop builder" (objective)
- Not "faster" (relative) but "results in 30 seconds" (specific)

### Step 3: Map Attributes to Value

**Question:** "So what? Why does this attribute matter to customers?"

| Unique Attribute | Value (Benefit) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| AI-powered analysis | Results in minutes, not weeks |
| No-code builder | Marketing team can self-serve without dev help |
| Pay-per-use pricing | No wasted spend on unused licenses |
| Industry-specific templates | Faster time to value, less customization |

**The "So what?" test:** For each attribute, ask "so what?" until you reach a business outcome the customer cares about.

### Step 4: Identify Your Best-Fit Customers

**Question:** "Who cares the MOST about your unique value?"

**Not your total addressable market.** Your best-fit customers are:
- The ones who buy fastest
- The ones who pay the most
- The ones who churn the least
- The ones who refer others

**Characteristics to look for:**
- Specific role or title
- Company size or stage
- Industry or vertical
- Situation or trigger event
- Current solution they're using

**Example:** Instead of "small businesses," → "Series A SaaS startups with 10-50 employees who just hired their first marketing manager and are currently doing analytics in spreadsheets."

### Step 5: Choose Your Market Category

**Question:** "What frame of reference helps customers understand your value fastest?"

**Three options:**

| Strategy | When to Use | Pros | Cons |
|----------|-------------|------|------|
| **Existing category** | Customers know the category | Easy to understand, existing demand | Compared to incumbents |
| **Sub-category** | You serve a specific niche | Focused, less competition | Smaller market perception |
| **New category** | Nothing like you exists | Own the category, no comparison | Must educate the market |

**Decision guide:**
- Is there an existing category people search for? → Use it
- Are you 10x better for a specific segment? → Create a sub-category
- Does your product enable something previously impossible? → New category (rare, risky)

## Positioning Statement Template

After completing all 5 steps:

```
For [best-fit customer]
Who [key problem/situation]
[Product] is a [market category]
That [key value/benefit].
Unlike [competitive alternative],
We [key differentiator mapped to value].
```

**Example:**
"For Series A SaaS startups who just hired their first marketing manager, DataDash is a marketing analytics platform that gives you board-ready reports in 30 seconds. Unlike spreadsheet-based tracking, we automatically pull data from all your tools and surface the metrics investors actually ask about."

## Positioning vs. Messaging

| Positioning | Messaging |
|-------------|-----------|
| Strategic decision | Tactical execution |
| Internal alignment | External communication |
| Changes rarely | Changes per channel/audience |
| "We are X for Y" | "Here's why you should care" |

**Positioning comes first.** Messaging flows from positioning. Never write copy before positioning is locked.

## Testing Your Positioning

**Quick test — The bar test:**
Imagine explaining your product to a smart friend at a bar. If they:
- Immediately understand what it is → Good positioning
- Ask "so it's like X but for Y?" → They're categorizing you (good)
- Look confused or ask "but what do you actually DO?" → Bad positioning

**Quantitative test:**
- Sales cycle length (shorter = better positioning)
- "What do you think we do?" survey to new visitors
- Win rate against specific competitors
- First-call conversion rate

## Common Positioning Mistakes

1. **Positioning for everyone** — "We're for businesses of all sizes." This means you're for nobody.
2. **Leading with features** — Nobody cares about features. They care about outcomes.
3. **Comparing to the wrong alternative** — If customers would use spreadsheets, don't compare to Salesforce.
4. **Category confusion** — Creating a new category when an existing one would work fine.
5. **Set it and forget it** — Positioning should be reviewed when you add major features, enter new markets, or see competitive shifts.
6. **Consensus-driven positioning** — Positioning is a strategic choice, not a committee decision. One person should own it.
