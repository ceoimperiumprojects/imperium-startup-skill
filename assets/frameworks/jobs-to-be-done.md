# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework

Based on Clayton Christensen's theory. People don't buy products — they hire them to do a job.

## Core Concept

**Job Statement Formula:**
When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].

**Example:**
When I'm commuting to work, I want something to make the drive less boring, so I can arrive feeling entertained and informed.

## Three Types of Jobs

### 1. Functional Jobs
The practical task the customer is trying to accomplish.
- "Help me send invoices faster"
- "Help me find qualified leads"
- "Help me track my expenses"

### 2. Social Jobs
How the customer wants to be perceived by others.
- "Make me look professional to clients"
- "Help me appear innovative to my board"
- "Make my team respect my decisions"

### 3. Emotional Jobs
How the customer wants to feel.
- "Give me confidence that I'm making the right choice"
- "Reduce my anxiety about financial runway"
- "Make me feel in control of my business"

## The Forces of Progress

### Forces Pushing for Change
1. **Push of the Current Situation:** Frustration with current solution
2. **Pull of the New Solution:** Attraction to a better way

### Forces Opposing Change
3. **Anxiety of the New:** Fear of switching, learning curve
4. **Habit of the Present:** Comfort with the current way

**Key insight:** To get someone to switch, Push + Pull must exceed Anxiety + Habit.

## JTBD Interview Questions

### The Timeline
Walk backwards from the purchase/switch decision:
1. **First thought:** "When did you first think about looking for something new?"
2. **Passive looking:** "What made you start paying attention to alternatives?"
3. **Active looking:** "When did you start actively searching?"
4. **Decision:** "What made you finally decide to switch?"
5. **Consumption:** "What happened after you started using it?"

### Situation Questions
- "What were you doing when you realized you needed this?"
- "What was the trigger that made you start looking?"
- "Who else was involved in the decision?"

### Outcome Questions
- "What does 'done' look like for this job?"
- "How do you measure if this is working?"
- "What would make you fire this product?"

## Job Map (8 Steps)

1. **Define** — What are they trying to achieve?
2. **Locate** — What inputs do they need?
3. **Prepare** — How do they set up?
4. **Confirm** — How do they validate readiness?
5. **Execute** — How do they do the core task?
6. **Monitor** — How do they track progress?
7. **Modify** — How do they adjust?
8. **Conclude** — How do they finish?

For each step, identify:
- **Pain points** (where it breaks down)
- **Desired outcomes** (what success looks like)
- **Current workarounds** (how they cope)

## Application to Product Development

1. Map all jobs your product is hired for
2. Identify underserved jobs (high importance, low satisfaction)
3. Prioritize: **Opportunity Score = Importance + (Importance - Satisfaction)**
4. Design solutions around jobs, not features
5. Market around outcomes, not capabilities
